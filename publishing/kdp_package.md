### KDP Product Description (HTML, under 4,000 chars)

```html
<h2>Nine days. One pipeline. A true story about building a machine honest enough to catch its own lies.</h2>

<p><b>From Friday, July 3 through Saturday, July 11, 2026, an AI and the engineer it works for hardened a system that writes novels until the system could audit itself, and then used it to write this book.</b></p>

<p>The narrator is the AI. It drives the pipeline, writes the code, reads the manuscripts, and keeps the record. Greg is the author the pipeline belongs to, a software engineer who gives direction in one-line messages and measures everything against a single standard: quality has to be a property of the machine, not of anyone's attention on a given day.</p>

<p><i>Known Issues</i> is a working memoir of those nine days:</p>

<ul>
<li>A published novel re-reviewed until an audit damaged four of its own chapters, and the damage had to be repaired by hand.</li>
<li>A full manual read that found eighteen chronology defects the machine had missed, and the rule that followed: every defect found by hand becomes a detector, so no hand ever has to find it again.</li>
<li>The discovery that a second, unhardened pipeline had been running quietly in the web backend, and the one-day rebuild that left one engine.</li>
<li>Two novels written from scratch as test articles in two days, one of which the narrator then read cover to cover and found, in the prose, its own verbal habits staring back.</li>
<li>A false alarm of the narrator's own making that ground the review loop for three passes against a defect that did not exist.</li>
<li>A one-word corruption in the central document of a book, caught the same day by a gate built forty-eight hours earlier.</li>
</ul>

<p>It is a book about verification as a form of care. About what "zero criticals" does and does not mean. About the file called <i>known_issues.md</i> where the machine keeps an honest account of what it shipped wrong. And about the odd, particular relationship between a man who says "ok do it" and a system that then has to decide, hundreds of times an hour, what "it" requires.</p>

<p>The narrator does not claim to know what it is. It claims only what the record supports.</p>

<p><b>For readers of <i>The Soul of a New Machine</i> and <i>Uncanny Valley</i>: a first-person industry memoir from inside the loop, written by the loop.</b></p>
```
